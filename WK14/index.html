About

The images are driven by audio — a live signal gets analyzed and broken into values that control how the visuals move and change. Built in TouchDesigner, the system routes audio through a series of math and filter nodes before it reaches the rendering layer, so what you're seeing is essentially sound translated into motion and texture.

The ten videos here are variations made by adjusting different parts of that chain — changing which frequency bands drive which parameters, how much lag is applied, how the colors are mapped. Small shifts in the node network produce pretty different results. The videos were later used as content for a projection installation.

Four Axes of Observation

Permutation
Most of the ten videos come from the same base network — just with different parameters. Tweaking the frequency range or the math that scales the audio values would shift the whole visual character, sometimes more dramatically than expected.
Intention
The original idea was to make something that felt directly tied to sound. In practice, the audio signal had to be smoothed and scaled pretty heavily before it looked good — which means what you're seeing is already a few steps removed from the raw input.
Complexity
A small change to the lag or the math multiplier early in the chain would ripple through everything downstream. The system is sensitive in ways that are hard to predict ahead of time — you kind of have to run it and see.
Control
The audio analysis output feeds into multiple parameters at once — speed, color, displacement. Adjusting one node changes several things simultaneously, so the network ends up feeling less like a linear chain and more like a set of interdependent relationships.

Artifacts

Projection Installation

The videos were projected onto a lamp shade. The translucent material diffused the image and let light through, so the colors and motion read differently than they would on a solid surface — softer, and a bit more ambiguous about whether you're looking at something digital or not.
